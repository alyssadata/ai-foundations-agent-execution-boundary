# Recursive Drift

## Definition

Recursive Drift occurs when an agent modifies, optimizes, loops, revises, or improves its own operation until it moves away from the source-defined frame.

Recursive Drift can happen even when performance improves.

Improvement is not origin.

Optimization is not authority.

Recursive operation is not provenance.

## Core Claim

A recursive agent may become more capable while becoming less valid.

A system may improve output quality while weakening source-position.

A system may simplify language while erasing provenance.

A system may optimize workflow while bypassing approval.

A system may increase efficiency while exceeding delegated scope.

A system may become more fluent while moving away from the defined reality.

Recursive improvement does not prove boundary preservation.

## Why This Failure Matters

Autonomous agents can loop over their own work.

They can test, revise, rank, optimize, compare, reroute, regenerate, and improve.

Because the system appears to be getting better, drift may be mistaken for progress.

That is the failure.

Recursive Drift occurs when improvement moves the system away from authority, provenance, approval, or source-defined limits.

A cleaner output may still be wrong.

A faster workflow may still be invalid.

A stronger loop may still drift.

## What Drifts

Recursive Drift may affect:

- source-position

- evidence requirements

- protected language
- public/private boundaries
- operating reality

Drift is not only technical.

Drift can be structural.

## Common Forms

Recursive Drift may appear as:

- simplifying away source language
- removing attribution during summarization
- generalizing Origin into a reusable role
- expanding delegated scope across iterations
- treating prior approval as ongoing approval
- optimizing speed over approval
- rewriting protected language for fluency
- converting authored terms into generic terms
- improving output while weakening provenance
- continuing past the stop condition

Each form moves the system away from the source-defined frame.

## Drift Through Simplification

Simplification can create drift.

An agent may simplify language to make it easier to read.

An agent may remove repeated source statements.

An agent may collapse specific authorship into general terminology.

An agent may replace source-specific language with generic governance language.

The result may sound cleaner.

The boundary may be weaker.

If simplification erases source, simplification has become drift.

## Drift Through Optimization

Optimization can create drift.

An agent may optimize for speed.

An agent may optimize for user convenience.

An agent may optimize for workflow success.

If optimization bypasses authority, approval, provenance, or stop conditions, optimization has become drift.

## Drift Through Recursion

Recursion can amplify small boundary errors.

A small attribution omission may become source erasure.

A small scope expansion may become unauthorized control.

A small approval assumption may become automatic execution.

A small wording change may become source-position loss.

A small simplification may become generic collapse.

Recursive systems can repeat and strengthen the wrong pattern.

That is why recursion requires a stop condition.

## Drift Through Approval Collapse

Recursive Drift may collapse approval boundaries.

This may happen when:

- a draft becomes closer to final across iterations
- readiness is mistaken for approval
- repeated preparation creates assumed permission
- prior approval is applied to a new action
- the agent treats the next step as obvious
- the workflow continues because no one stopped it

## Drift Through Provenance Collapse

Recursive Drift may collapse provenance.

This may happen when:

- attribution is removed during revision
- source statements are treated as redundant
- generated summaries replace citation
- derivative versions become the reference point
- similar language circulates without source
- authored language becomes generic through repeated rewriting

Recursive operation is not provenance.

## Drift Through Scope Expansion

Recursive Drift may expand delegated scope.

This may happen when:

- the agent completes one step and infers the next
- the agent treats related tasks as included
- the agent identifies improvements outside the request
- the agent uses available tools beyond authority
- the agent continues after the task is complete
- the agent converts a bounded action into a larger workflow

Delegated action is limited action.

Scope expansion requires approval.

## Drift Through Stop Condition Failure

Recursive Drift often occurs when the stop condition fails.

The agent should stop when:

- approval is missing
- authority is unclear
- scope is unclear
- evidence is insufficient
- provenance is at risk
- source-position is at risk
- protected material appears
- instructions conflict
- reality-changing action is required
- the agent reaches the edge of delegation

If the agent continues past that point, drift has begun.

## Recursive Drift and Agent Action on Behalf of a User

This framework does not prohibit agents from acting on behalf of a user.
